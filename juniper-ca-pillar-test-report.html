Section 05

Consumer Psychology & Learnings

What the performance data reveals about how this audience thinks, where they are in their decision journey, and what moves them to act.

Decision state

This audience is in a decision loop, not a discovery loop

The top-performing copy doesn't educate — it unsticks. "What are you actually waiting for?" assumes the reader already knows about GLP-1s and has been sitting on it. The data confirms this: friction-removal language ("that's the whole risk", "$0 assessment", "5 minutes") outperforms awareness-building copy. The job is not to introduce an idea, it's to give permission to act.

Trust barrier

Scepticism is an identity, not just an objection

"It makes sense to be suspicious" converts because it reflects back how this person sees themselves — as someone who doesn't get fooled. Leading with "you're right to doubt this" is qualitatively different from reassurance. It compliments their judgment before selling to them. Copy that leads with Juniper's credentials or features triggers the exact skepticism it's trying to overcome.

System frustration

The Canadian healthcare system is a live grievance

The GP critique ("10-minute appointments", "told you to eat less and move more") resonates because it's lived experience. Universal healthcare means this audience has had the public system dismiss them, repeatedly. Positioning Juniper as an alternative to a system that has failed them is more emotionally charged than positioning against a competitor.

Risk framing

Risk mitigation needs to be explicit, not implied

The best-performing copy names the risk and shrinks it in one move: "The consultation is free. The quiz is 5 minutes. That's the whole risk." Burying "free assessment" in the CTA is not the same thing. This audience needs the cost of trying to feel genuinely negligible — stated directly, not just structurally available. Risk removal should appear throughout the funnel, not only in the headline.

Emotional stage

"It's not your fault" validates but doesn't convert

Empathy-led copy ("You've done the hard part: realising it's not your fault") drives quiz starts but not purchases. It releases shame and leaves people in a reflective emotional state — but reflection is not the same as readiness. Copy that validates and immediately pivots to action ("here's the one thing you haven't tried yet") is the gap to close.

Message timing

The "beyond medication" message is mistimed in cold prospecting

The high hold rates (34–40%) on "what GLP-1s won't tell you" and "4.5x more weight" copy show deep reading — but no purchases follow. The message creates complexity before the primary decision is made: "do I want to start?". Telling someone the medication alone isn't enough, before they've committed to starting, introduces doubt about the category. This message belongs in retargeting, not cold prospecting.

Creative format

Authentic UGC stops the scroll better than anything else

JUN115 Car Story A has a 48.8% hook rate — nearly half of everyone who sees it stops scrolling. Car Story B drives the highest CTR (2.52%) and best quiz starts in the campaign. Talking-to-camera UGC is outperforming all other formats on attention metrics. This audience trusts real people talking to camera over produced creative.

Avatar specificity

Specific biographies exclude; emotional truths include

"Two kids. Full-time job." is well-written but self-selecting. Anyone without kids, or who works differently, disengages in the first sentence. The underlying emotional truth ("at some point you just accepted this was what life looked like now") is universal. Strip the biography, keep the feeling. Broad resonance comes from emotional universality, not narrative specificity.

Composite audience portrait

A woman aged 38–55 who has tried multiple weight loss approaches and carries accumulated disappointment from each one. She is aware of GLP-1s — probably has researched Ozempic — but is in a paralysis loop, not a discovery phase. She is intellectually sceptical of anything that sounds promotional, particularly in health and wellness. She is frustrated with a public healthcare system that has given her generic advice for years. Her primary blockers are perceived cost and commitment risk, not lack of interest. The creative that wins is the creative that respects her intelligence, validates her scepticism as earned, strips every reason to hesitate, and tells her she's already made the decision — she just hasn't acted on it yet.

Section 06

Creative Direction & Feedback

Specific feedback on what's working, what to fix, and principles for the next round of creative.

What's working — keep & scale

Start inside the reader's head, not inside our product

The top-performing copy shares one structural quality: the opening line describes something the reader already feels. "What are you actually waiting for?" names a feeling. "It makes sense to be suspicious" reflects a thought back. This is the test for every new piece of copy: does the first sentence describe something the reader is already thinking, or does it describe something we want them to know? The former converts. The latter doesn't.

The "what are you waiting for?" body + "Free to See If You Qualify" headline is the benchmark combination: 2.32% CTR, 38.4% hook, $26 CPReg. Every new variant should be compared against this.

What's working — replicate the mechanism

Name the risk, then collapse it completely

"That's the whole risk" is the strongest close in this campaign. It doesn't say "low risk" — it names the specific risk and declares it negligible. This is different from listing features ("$0 assessment, no clinic visit") because it acknowledges the reader's hesitation explicitly before dismissing it.

Every piece of copy needs a version of this moment before the CTA. Not "free to see if you're eligible" as a throwaway — a direct, named articulation of what the reader is risking by clicking, and why it's nothing.

What to fix — specific issues

The feature-led and emoji copy isn't working

The "✅ Practitioner support isn't a slogan. It's a person" copy had the worst CTR in the campaign. The problem isn't the emoji alone — it's that the whole piece reads like a product brochure. It opens with our feature, defends it against an objection nobody raised, then lists more features.

This audience has been sold to before by companies making exactly these claims. When you open with credentials, you're asking them to evaluate you. When you open with their experience, you're asking them to recognise themselves. These are different psychological entry points — one creates resistance, one creates connection.

What to fix — specific issues

Strip the biography from the "two kids, full-time job" copy

The emotional core of this piece is strong: "at some point you just accepted that this was what life looked like now." That line works for almost everyone in the cohort. The problem is getting there requires reading through a very specific life story that self-selects. Anyone without kids, or who doesn't identify with "zero bandwidth", disengages before they reach it.

Rewrite starting from the emotional truth: the exhaustion, the self-blame, the resigned acceptance. Let the audience fill in their own biography rather than giving them yours to reject.

What to fix — specific issues

The "beyond medication" copy is in the wrong place in the funnel

The 34–40% hold rates on "what GLP-1s won't tell you" and the 4.5x stat copy are genuine signals — people are reading deeply. But the message is creating complexity at the wrong moment. If someone hasn't fully committed to starting GLP-1s, telling them "the medication alone isn't enough" introduces doubt about the whole category, not just other providers.

Commission retargeting versions of these concepts: "You've started looking into this — here's why how you do it matters." That's a completely different frame from trying to convince someone to start. In cold prospecting, these concepts should be reserved for the hold/engagement role while a stronger direct-response close carries the conversion.

Direction for next round

Fix the Car Story A edit — the hook is already solved

JUN115 Car Story A has a 48.8% hook rate — the best of any ad in this campaign. Nearly half of everyone who sees it stops scrolling. But hold drops to 10.6%, meaning almost everyone leaves within 3–5 seconds. This is not a concept problem, it's an edit problem. Something immediately after the opening visual or first line is losing people.

Don't brief a new concept here — brief a new cut. Find what's happening at the 3–5 second mark and remove or rework it. The front of this ad is exceptional creative that shouldn't be wasted.

Direction for next round

Lean into scepticism as the primary opener

The "it makes sense to be suspicious" copy converts well on limited spend and has the right mechanism. Brief three new variations of this structure: acknowledge the earned cynicism, validate it specifically (name what has failed before), then position Juniper as different in one concrete, provable way. The key is that the acknowledgment must feel genuine, not performative — if it reads like a sales technique, it will backfire with exactly this audience.

"Another weight loss program. You've heard that before. So have we. Here's the one thing that's actually different."

Test variations that name the specific things that have failed (meal plans, GPs, willpower, calorie counting) to increase recognition before pivoting to the solution.

Direction for next round

Build the "System Failed You" pillar into the GP indictment angle

The current "System Failed You" ads have a $1,544 CPP — but the strongest headline in the account ("Built for What 10-Minute Appointments Miss") comes from this concept space and drives meaningful quiz starts. The pillar has audience resonance but the conversion close is failing. The GP critique creates anger and recognition; what's missing is the immediate redirect from that anger to the action. The gap is: I'm angry at my GP → what do I do right now? Brief copy that builds the frustration and then closes with total immediacy and simplicity.

Summary principles for next brief

Start with their experience. If the first sentence describes Juniper, a feature, or a statistic — rewrite it. The opening must make the reader think "yes, exactly."

Name the scepticism explicitly. "It makes sense to be suspicious" outperforms "Juniper is different" — even though both make the same claim — because one earns credibility and one asserts it.

Collapse the risk directly. "That's the whole risk" is a model. Find five ways to write that sentence for the next batch.

The "beyond meds" message goes in retargeting. Commission separate retargeting copy for this concept — it's the right message at the wrong funnel stage.

Emotional universality over biography. Strip specific life circumstances. Keep emotional truths. The reader fills in their own story.

Sound like a person, not a brand. The copy that converts has a point of view. Safe, approved, hedged copy reads like it, and this audience rejects it.
